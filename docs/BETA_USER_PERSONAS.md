# Chadwit Beta User Personas

Three brutally realistic beta user personas for stress-testing Chadwit NYC: where users lose trust, where onboarding breaks, where the directory feels thin, and where the product fails to beat Google or earn a return visit. Tone: direct, skeptical, actionable.

---

## 1. Negative User — Quickly Frustrated, Low Patience, Churns Fast

### Profile

- **Full name:** Marcus Webb
- **Age:** 28
- **NYC location:** Crown Heights, Brooklyn
- **Background / life stage:** High school history teacher. Previously volunteered with a food bank in college. Wants a local weekend or after-school volunteer slot with no long commitment. Time-poor, goal-oriented.
- **Technical ability:** Comfortable with apps and the web; expects sites to work on his phone and to find what he needs in a couple of minutes.
- **Primary device:** iPhone; uses it for most research and planning.

### What they need

- **Problem:** Find 2–3 real volunteer options in Brooklyn (or nearby) with working links and clear next steps.
- **What they hoped Chadwit would do:** Be a usable list he can filter by area and type, with links that go to real volunteer or contact pages—better than a random Google search.
- **Minimum value to come back:** At least one org he successfully contacts or applies to; links that work; enough Brooklyn results that the product doesn’t feel broken.

### Behavioral reality

- **How they browse:** Short bursts (10–15 min). Goes straight to filters; rarely reads FAQ or “How to use” on first visit. Scrolls past nav and tagline.
- **Time before deciding:** About 3–5 minutes. If the first few clicks don’t pay off, he’s out.
- **What they click first:** Borough filter, then a “Top recommended” or first card—Website or Volunteer.
- **FAQ:** Ignores it unless something is clearly broken or confusing; then skims for “confirm with the organization” and feels deflected.
- **Friction tolerance:** Very low. One dead or misleading link is enough to question the whole directory; two and he’s gone.

### Main failure mode

**“Too many dead ends—and not enough useful organizations.”** The directory sends him to a generic contact page when he clicks Volunteer, or to an “under construction” website. The list for Brooklyn is short with no explanation, so he can’t tell if the product is thin or broken. Combined, it feels like more effort than Googling.

### Trust test

To trust Chadwit he needs:

- **Visible “last checked” (or “updated”) on every card** so he knows how fresh the data is.
- **One-line explanation of confidence and status** above or beside the list (no FAQ dive)—e.g. “Confidence = how recently we verified; status = active / seeking volunteers.”
- **Working volunteer links** that go to a real volunteer/opportunity page, not a generic contact form with no volunteer info.
- **Short, visible copy** that the list is curated and growing—so a short Brooklyn list feels intentional, not broken.
- He does **not** need an About page on first visit; he needs results and working links. If those fail, About won’t save him.

### Drop-off threshold

- **Broken or misleading links:** One is a warning; two and he leaves. “Volunteer” → generic contact with no volunteer info counts as misleading.
- **Minutes given:** ~5 max on first session. If he hasn’t found one plausible org to contact in that time, he’s done.
- **Relevant listings expected:** At least 5–8 for Brooklyn (or a clear note that coverage is limited and growing). Fewer than that with no explanation = “this doesn’t have my area.”
- **Exact abandonment moment:** Second bad click-through (wrong page or dead link), or after scrolling the short Brooklyn list with no “curated and growing” message.
- **Return without a prompt:** Unlikely. He might revisit once if he submitted feedback, to see if anything changed. No reply or visible fix = no second visit.

### Likely criticism

- “Brooklyn has almost nothing and one of the links was broken and one Volunteer button went to a generic contact page. I don’t know when this was last updated or who runs it. For the time I spent I could’ve just Googled.”
- “What does ‘Checked’ mean? Why would I turn on low-confidence? I’m not reading the FAQ to figure that out.”
- “I’d use it again if the links actually worked and there was more in my area—and if I knew it was maintained.”

### Quote

> “I picked Brooklyn and got like six things. One sent me to an ‘under construction’ page and another had a Volunteer button that went to a generic contact form. I don’t know who runs this or when it was last updated. If I’m going to spend time on this, I need the links to work and a reason to use it instead of Google.”

### Step-by-step failure scenario

1. **Where they came from:** Colleague’s link in a group chat—“new NYC volunteer directory.” Opens on his phone.
2. **Filters:** Category = “Community & Immigrant Support”; first leaves borough on “All boroughs,” then switches to **Brooklyn** when he sees most results are Manhattan/Queens.
3. **What they clicked:** Two “Top recommended,” then first Brooklyn org **Website**, then **Volunteer**. Volunteer → generic contact page, no volunteer info. Second org **Website** → “under construction.” Closes tab.
4. **Confusion / disappointment:** Short Brooklyn list with no “curated and growing” note; “Include low-confidence” with no inline explanation; FAQ “confirm with the organization” feels like deflection; contact form in a new tab feels disconnected and he’s not sure anyone will respond.
5. **Feedback:** Might submit one contact form with a broken link and wrong volunteer target. If no reply or no visible fix, won’t bother again.
6. **Why they left:** Second dead/misleading link + thin Brooklyn results with no context. Concluded the product isn’t worth the effort vs. Google.

### What this persona is really testing

- **Link reliability** (Volunteer and Website)
- **Borough coverage perception** (thin list + no explanation)
- **Plain-language clarity** of confidence/status without FAQ
- **Time-to-first-useful-org** and **relevant results per session**
- **Return likelihood** after one bad session

### Product implication

- **What Chadwit must fix to win this user:** Reliable links (audit featured and high-confidence; prefer Volunteer → real opportunity page); visible “last checked” on cards; one-line confidence/status explanation above or beside the list; short “curated and growing” copy when results are few; respond to feedback when possible.
- **Recoverable?** Only if fixes are visible quickly (e.g. reply to his correction, 1–2 link fixes, clearer copy). Otherwise he’s gone.
- **Could recommend?** Not in current state. If fixed: possibly—“it’s small but the links work and they have Brooklyn.”

## 2. Skeptical User — Cautious, Trust-Sensitive, Hard to Convince

### Profile

- **Full name:** Priya Sharma
- **Age:** 34
- **NYC location:** Astoria, Queens
- **Background / life stage:** Immigration attorney at a mid-size firm. Works with nonprofits and community orgs; has seen poorly maintained “curated” lists and directories that are outdated or biased. Cares who stands behind the information.
- **Technical ability:** Strong. Uses legal databases, spreadsheets, multiple browsers; reads Terms and Privacy when trying a new service that might touch client or professional use.
- **Primary device:** Laptop for serious research; phone for quick checks.

### What they need

- **Problem:** Reliable referral list for immigration/community support in Queens and NYC that she can recommend to clients or colleagues with confidence.
- **What they hoped Chadwit would do:** Be transparent about who runs it, how orgs are chosen, and how often info is updated—so “curated” and “Top recommended” mean something.
- **Minimum value to come back:** Clear About / How we curate; visible “last checked” and confidence; proof that corrections are reviewed (e.g. response or visible update); no overclaiming.

### Behavioral reality

- **How they browse:** Reads tagline and header, then **How to use** and **FAQ** before diving into filters. Looks for About, Terms, Privacy. Scans cards for “last checked” and confidence.
- **Time before deciding:** 10–15 minutes. Will give detailed feedback if she thinks the product can improve.
- **What they click first:** How to use, FAQ, then filters (Category = Community & Immigrant Support, Borough = Queens, Type = Direct Organization). Then Website/Volunteer on 2–3 orgs to verify links.
- **FAQ:** Reads it. Notes “we don’t guarantee” and confidence explanation. Looks for who runs it and how often updates happen—doesn’t find it.
- **Friction tolerance:** Moderate for UX; very low for opacity. Will tolerate a small list if transparency and follow-through are clear. Will abandon if she can’t answer “who runs this and do they maintain it?”

### Main failure mode

**“Not enough proof this is maintained—and too opaque.”** No About or “How we curate”; “last checked” is buried in card meta; contact is a Google Form with no “we review submissions” or response-time expectation. “Curated” and “Top recommended” feel unanchored. She can’t recommend Chadwit to clients or colleagues until she knows who’s behind it and that feedback is taken seriously.

### Trust test

She needs:

- **About / How we curate:** Who maintains the list; that it’s curated and not comprehensive; that listings are reviewed periodically (even approximate); that suggestions/corrections are welcome and how they’re handled.
- **“Last checked” (or “Info verified”) visible on every card**, with confidence explained in one line near the list or in How to use.
- **Contact expectations:** One line on the form or footer—e.g. “We review submissions regularly” or “We aim to respond to corrections within a few days.” No need for an SLA; just a signal that someone reads and acts.
- **Reliable volunteer/website links** so the directory doesn’t look abandoned. Fewer “Coming soon” items in Resources; prefer fewer, finished items over many placeholders.
- **Clear, honest disclaimer** (already present)—and no overclaiming (“best list,” “every vetted org”).

### Drop-off threshold

- **Broken links:** One or two wrong/outdated links are tolerable if there’s a clear way to report and she sees a response. No response = “not maintained.”
- **Minutes given:** 10–15 on first visit; will check back in a week if she submitted a correction.
- **Relevant listings:** Will accept limited Queens coverage if transparency is there. Expects Legal & Housing and Community & Immigrant Support to have some depth.
- **Abandonment moment:** After reading FAQ and footer and still having no answer to “who runs this?” or after submitting a correction and getting no reply or no visible change.
- **Return without a prompt:** Yes, if she bookmarked it and sees a clear improvement (About, “last checked” prominence, or a human response to her feedback).

### Likely criticism

- “Add an About page—who runs this and how often do you update? I’d put ‘last checked’ on the card more prominently; that’s what I look for.”
- “The contact form is fine, but add one line that you review corrections so people know what to expect. Resources: either ship fewer items or don’t show so many ‘Coming soon’; it looks unfinished.”
- “I like that you’re explicit about not guaranteeing legitimacy and that you have confidence levels. I don’t see who’s behind this or how often you actually update. If I’m going to point clients or colleagues here, I need to know it’s maintained and that when I send a correction, someone will look at it.”

### Quote

> “I like that you’re explicit about not guaranteeing legitimacy and that you have confidence levels. What I don’t see is who’s behind this and how often you actually update. If I’m going to point clients or colleagues here, I need to know it’s maintained and that when I send a correction, someone will look at it. Right now it feels careful but a bit opaque.”

### Step-by-step failure scenario

1. **Where they came from:** Post in a professional Slack (immigration / nonprofit) about Chadwit. Opens on laptop with low expectations.
2. **Filters:** Reads How to use and FAQ first. Looks for About—doesn’t find it. Sets Category = Community & Immigrant Support, Borough = Queens, Type = Direct Organization.
3. **What they clicked:** “Top recommended” and a few cards; looks for “last checked” and confidence—finds them in meta but thinks they should be more prominent. Clicks Website and Volunteer on two orgs; both work. Bookmarks Chadwit but notes limited Queens and opacity.
4. **Confusion / disappointment:** No About; “last checked” not prominent; Resources has several “Coming soon” → feels over-promised or understaffed; Terms/Privacy skimmed, contact form has no response-time or “we review” note.
5. **Feedback:** Submits a small correction (e.g. typo or URL) and a note: “Suggest adding when you last review listings and whether you respond to corrections.” Will check back in a week.
6. **Why they stayed or left:** Stays as a possible user if she gets a clear, human response and sees a small improvement (e.g. “last checked” more visible or an About sentence). Leaves mentally if no response or generic reply—treats Chadwit as well-intentioned but unmaintained.

### What this persona is really testing

- **Trust and transparency** (About, how we curate, who runs it)
- **Visibility of “last checked” and confidence**
- **Evidence that feedback is reviewed** (contact expectations, response)
- **Professional usability** (clear categories, working links, no overclaiming)
- **Return and recommend** after transparency and follow-through

### Product implication

- **What Chadwit must fix to win this user:** Minimal About / How we curate; “last checked” visible on every card; one-line contact expectation (“We review submissions regularly” / “We aim to respond to corrections within a few days”); respond to at least one correction with a human reply; trim or relabel “Coming soon” in Resources.
- **Recoverable?** Yes. She’s willing to use a small, honest directory that states its limits and keeps data reasonably current. One good response and visible transparency can turn her into a recommender.
- **Could recommend?** Yes, if transparency and follow-through improve. She may share Chadwit in her professional network with a caveat (“small list, but maintained and clear about what it is”).

## 3. Hopeful but Fragile User — Wants Help Badly, Confusion or Friction Makes Them Give Up

### Profile

- **Full name:** Yuki Tanaka
- **Age:** 22
- **NYC location:** East Harlem, Manhattan (recent transplant)
- **Background / life stage:** Recent grad, first year in NYC. Needs to build community and maybe volunteer for resume and mental health. Anxious about “doing it wrong”; prefers clear steps and low commitment. English is strong but not native; gets stressed by jargon and long blocks of text.
- **Technical ability:** Uses phone for everything; comfortable with apps but easily put off by unclear labels or too many options.
- **Primary device:** iPhone.

### What they need

- **Problem:** Find 1–2 safe, low-commitment ways to get involved (volunteer or newcomer-friendly org) near them or accessible by transit. Needs to feel it’s “for people like me” and that the next step is obvious.
- **What they hoped Chadwit would do:** Feel like a friendly, clear guide—not another overwhelming or corporate-feeling directory. Simple filters, plain language, and links that lead to a real “how to get involved” page.
- **Minimum value to come back:** At least one org that feels relevant and reachable; no scary dead ends or “under construction” pages; labels (confidence, status, recommended) that make sense without a manual.

### Behavioral reality

- **How they browse:** Scrolls quickly; looks for something that “fits” (location, vibe, low commitment). May not understand Category or Type at first; relies on borough and “Top recommended.”
- **Time before deciding:** 5–8 minutes. If they feel confused or that they’re “not the right user,” they’ll leave. If they hit a dead end, they’re more likely to give up than to report it.
- **What they click first:** Borough (Manhattan or NYC-wide), then “Top recommended” or the first card that sounds welcoming. Clicks Volunteer or Website hoping for a clear next step.
- **FAQ:** Doesn’t read it unless something goes wrong. If they do, long answers and terms like “curated,” “confidence,” “status” add to confusion. “Confirm with the organization” and “we don’t guarantee” can feel scary rather than reassuring.
- **Friction tolerance:** Low. Unclear labels, empty or thin results with no explanation, or one dead link can trigger “this isn’t for me” or “I’ll try later” (and they don’t).

### Main failure mode

**“Too confusing for a stressed newcomer—and too many dead ends.”** They don’t know what “Checked,” “Active,” “Opportunity Open,” or “confidence” mean without opening the FAQ; “Include low-confidence” sounds like “show me worse options.” When they click Volunteer they land on a generic contact page or a broken site and don’t know if they did something wrong or if the directory is outdated. No “curated and growing” message when the list is short makes them think they’re in the wrong place. They leave not angry, but deflated—and don’t return unless reminded.

### Trust test

They need:

- **Plain-language copy:** One short sentence each for confidence and status near the list (e.g. “We recently verified this listing” / “This org is actively looking for volunteers”). No jargon; no “opt in to low-confidence” without a simple explanation.
- **Visible “last checked”** so they feel the list is cared for (even if they don’t parse dates carefully).
- **Reliable volunteer links** that go to a real “get involved” or application page—not a generic contact form with no volunteer info. One bad experience undermines trust.
- **Short reassurance when results are few:** e.g. “This list is curated and we’re adding more. Fewer results here doesn’t mean there aren’t great orgs—we’re still growing.” Reduces “am I in the wrong place?”
- **About / How we curate:** Simple “who we are and what we do” so the product doesn’t feel anonymous. One short paragraph is enough.
- **FAQ:** Same info in simpler, shorter form; “we don’t guarantee” paired with “always confirm with the org” in a friendly tone so it doesn’t feel alarming.

### Drop-off threshold

- **Broken or misleading links:** One is enough to make them doubt. They’re unlikely to report it; they’ll just leave.
- **Minutes given:** 5–8. If they don’t see a clear path to one org in that window, they’ll bounce or “come back later” (often never).
- **Relevant listings expected:** At least 3–5 that feel relevant (Manhattan or NYC-wide, newcomer- or volunteer-friendly). Empty or very thin list with no explanation = “this isn’t for me.”
- **Abandonment moment:** First confusing label they can’t decode quickly, or first click that leads to a dead/generic page. Also: scrolling to the bottom of a short list with no “we’re growing” message.
- **Return without a prompt:** Unlikely unless a friend or school reminds them, or they see the product again with a clearer value prop.

### Likely criticism

- “I wasn’t sure what ‘Checked’ or ‘confidence’ meant and I didn’t want to read a long FAQ. I clicked Volunteer on one that said it was recommended and it was just a contact form. I didn’t know if I did something wrong.”
- “When I picked Manhattan there weren’t that many and I didn’t know if that’s normal or if the site was incomplete. I left and figured I’d try Google.”
- “It seems nice but I got confused and then one link didn’t work. I’ll probably try again later.” (They rarely do.)

### Quote

> “I wanted to find something simple to get involved with. I saw ‘Top recommended’ and clicked one, but the Volunteer link just went to a contact page with no volunteer info. I didn’t know what ‘Checked’ or the confidence thing meant without opening the FAQ. I felt like I was missing something. I left and thought I’d come back later, but I haven’t.”

### Step-by-step failure scenario

1. **Where they came from:** Link from a “new in NYC” or grad-school group chat. Opens on phone.
2. **Filters:** Leaves category default or picks something broad (e.g. Community & Immigrant Support). Sets Borough to Manhattan. Sees “Top recommended” and a short list; doesn’t touch “Include low-confidence” because it sounds negative.
3. **What they clicked:** First “Top recommended” org → **Volunteer**. Lands on generic contact page, no volunteer info. Goes back; clicks **Website** on another org → “under construction” or slow/broken. Stops clicking.
4. **Confusion / disappointment:** “Checked,” “Active,” “confidence” on cards—no quick explanation. Short list with no “curated and growing” message. Feels like either the site is incomplete or they’re using it wrong. Doesn’t open contact form to report; doesn’t read full FAQ.
5. **Feedback:** Unlikely to submit unless prompted. If they do, might say “Volunteer link didn’t go to volunteer info” or “I was confused by the labels.”
6. **Why they left:** Combination of one bad link and unclear labels. Not angry—just deflated. “I’ll try later” with low likelihood of return.

### What this persona is really testing

- **Plain-language clarity** (confidence, status, recommended) without FAQ dependence
- **Onboarding and first-run experience** for a stressed, mobile-first user
- **Link reliability** (Volunteer and Website) for someone who won’t report errors
- **Thin-directory messaging** (curated and growing) so they don’t self-blame
- **Return likelihood** and **trust score after session** for a fragile but hopeful user

### Product implication

- **What Chadwit must fix to win this user:** One-line, plain-language explanation of confidence and status above or beside the list; visible “last checked”; audit Volunteer links so they go to real opportunity pages; short “curated and growing” when results are few; simple About so the product doesn’t feel anonymous; tone in FAQ and disclaimer that’s reassuring, not alarming.
- **Recoverable?** Yes, but only if the next touch (e.g. reminder from a community or school) brings them back to a clearer, more reliable experience. They won’t proactively return otherwise.
- **Could recommend?** Possibly to peers in “new in NYC” or student circles—but only after they’ve had one successful path to an org and the labels make sense. Right now they’re more likely to say “I tried it but got confused” or “one of the links didn’t work.”

# Hard Truths for Chadwit Before Wider Beta

## 1. The 5 Biggest Reasons Users May Bounce

1. **Dead or misleading links** — Volunteer → generic contact with no volunteer info, or Website → “under construction.” One is a warning; two and users leave. This beats “curated” and “Top recommended” in their mind.
2. **Thin directory with no explanation** — Few results per borough/category read as “broken” or “not for me.” Without “curated and growing” (or similar) copy, users assume the product is incomplete or irrelevant.
3. **Confidence and status unclear** — “Checked,” “Active,” “Opportunity Open,” “confidence,” and “Include low-confidence” are not self-explanatory. Burying the explanation in the FAQ means most users never get it and either guess or distrust.
4. **No proof of maintenance or ownership** — No About, no visible “last checked,” no contact expectations (“we review submissions”). Trust-sensitive and professional users won’t recommend or return until they know who runs it and that feedback is used.
5. **Too much effort for too little value** — When the directory doesn’t clearly beat a quick Google search (working links, clear next steps, enough relevant results), users conclude it’s not worth the time and don’t return.

## 2. Top Trust Failures

- **Opacity:** No About / How we curate; “last checked” buried in card meta.
- **No feedback loop:** Contact form with no “we review submissions” or response expectation; no reply to corrections → “nobody’s home.”
- **Labels without explanation:** Confidence and status only explained in FAQ; “Include low-confidence” sounds risky or negative without a one-line explainer.
- **Link quality:** Featured and high-confidence orgs with broken or misleading Volunteer/Website links undercut “curated” and “Top recommended.”
- **Over-promising:** Too many “Coming soon” items in Resources makes the product feel unfinished or overcommitted.

## 3. Fastest Wins to Improve Retention

- **Surface “last checked”** on every card (or “Info verified [date]”). Low effort, high trust impact.
- **One-line confidence and status** above or beside the first list view (and optionally next to filter labels). Reduces confusion and FAQ dependence.
- **Short “curated and growing” (or similar) copy** when result count is low. Sets expectation that a short list is intentional, not broken.
- **One sentence on the contact form or footer:** “We review submissions regularly and prioritize corrections.” Sets expectation; no SLA required.
- **Audit and fix** featured and high-confidence org links (especially Volunteer → real opportunity page). Stops the most damaging dead ends first.

## 4. What Must Be Fixed Before Inviting More Users

- **Link reliability:** At least featured and “Top recommended” orgs must have working Website and Volunteer links that go to the right kind of page. Add a simple “Report wrong link” or “Suggest correction” in the form description or on the card.
- **Confidence/status clarity:** One-line explanation visible without opening the FAQ. Consider a tooltip or help link next to the “Include low-confidence” checkbox and status filter.
- **Transparency:** Minimal About / How we curate (who maintains the list, that it’s curated and not comprehensive, that listings are reviewed periodically, that suggestions are welcome). Link from header or footer.
- **Thin-list messaging:** When filters return few results, show a short line that the directory is curated and growing so users don’t assume the product is broken or irrelevant.
- **Contact expectations:** One line that submissions are reviewed (and ideally that corrections are prioritized). Where possible, respond to at least one correction so early beta users see that feedback is used.

## 5. Metrics to Watch First

| Metric | Why it matters |
|--------|----------------|
| **Time to first useful org** | How long until the user finds one org they can actually use (click through to a real opportunity/contact). If this is high, drop-off will be high. |
| **Relevant results per session** | Count of results that match filters and that the user could reasonably consider. Thin sessions (0–2) without explanation drive “not for me” or “broken.” |
| **Dead or misleading link rate** | % of Website/Volunteer clicks that lead to dead, “under construction,” or wrong-type pages. Track by card and by confidence/featured. This is the fastest way to lose trust. |
| **Confidence/status confusion** | Proxy: FAQ opens on “confidence” or “status” questions; or support/contact mentions of “what does Checked mean.” Reduce by surfacing one-line explanation. |
| **FAQ dependence** | How often users open FAQ or How to use before or during directory use. High dependence for basic concepts = labels and copy need to move into the main flow. |
| **Contact form submissions** | Volume and type (correction vs. suggestion vs. general). Corrections are leading indicators of link and data quality; respond and track. |
| **Repeat visit intent** | Post-session or short survey: “Would you use Chadwit again?” or “Would you recommend it?” Correlate with link quality, clarity, and transparency. |
| **Trust score after session** | Simple 1–5 or “How much do you trust that the information here is current and useful?” Track before/after transparency and link-audit fixes. |
